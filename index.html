How I think

Four principles I don't negotiate on

These aren't values-wall platitudes. They're constraints I apply to every engineering decision, org design, and strategy conversation.

01 — SYSTEMS

Platforms are products, not utilities.

Internal developer experience is a competitive moat. I build platform teams with product thinking — roadmaps, SLAs, feedback loops — because the best infrastructure is the one engineers actually want to use.

02 — RELIABILITY

Reliability is a business outcome, not a technical metric.

Uptime means nothing without context. I tie SLOs to revenue impact, customer experience, and risk tolerance — then build the engineering culture to own those numbers without heroics.

03 — ORGANIZATION

Structure is strategy made visible.

Conway's Law is real. How you arrange your teams determines the systems they build. I design organizations the way I design architectures — with clear interfaces, minimal coupling, and explicit ownership.

04 — LEADERSHIP

The best technical leaders make themselves legible.

I translate between engineers and executives fluently — not by dumbing things down, but by finding the shared language of tradeoffs, risk, and outcome. Ambiguity is a leadership failure.

05 — GROWTH

Design the system before you manage people.

Most performance challenges are symptoms of poorly designed systems rather than individual shortcomings. Invest in organizational design, decision frameworks, feedback loops, and team culture so that great outcomes become the default.

06 — CHANGE

Transformation that doesn't move the business needle is theater.

I've learned that successful transformations aren't defined by migrations completed or frameworks adopted—they're defined by measurable business outcomes. The real proof is in faster delivery, improved reliability, lower operational friction, and sustained value for customers and the business.

Platform Engineering

The Silent Tax on Your Engineering Org — And How Platform Thinking Eliminates It

In one of my organisations, something caught my attention immediately. Talented engineers — senior ones — were spending their mornings waiting. Waiting for environments to spin up. Waiting for access approvals. Waiting for someone on a different team to run a deployment pipeline they didn’t own. Nobody called it a crisis. It didn’t show up in a P1 incident. But it was costing us thousands of hours every quarter. I call it the silent tax — the invisible overhead every engineering organization accumulates when platform infrastructure is treated as an afterthought rather than a product. “Platform debt is not a technical problem. It is an organizational strategy problem disguised as friction.”

Org Design

Your Org Chart Is Already Your Architecture Diagram

“Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization’s communication structure.” We call it Conway’s Law now. Most engineering leaders have heard of it. Far fewer have internalized what it actually implies: your org chart is not a description of how you build software. It is a forcing function that determines what you are architecturally capable of building. This article walks through three things — the mechanism behind the law, a practical way to diagnose where it’s hurting you, and a real transformation I led where acting on this deliberately, instead of accidentally, changed the numbers.

Stop Kubernetes-Washing Your Infrastructure: A Practical Guide to When It Helps (and When It Hurts)

There’s a pattern playing out across engineering organisations today. A team reads a case study from Netflix or Spotify. They attend KubeCon. Their CTO comes back from a conference with slides. Three months later, they’re wrestling with a 2,000-line Helm chart to deploy a monolith that serves 400 users. The engineers are exhausted. The deployment is slower than before. This is Kubernetes-washing: reaching for a tool because it signals operational sophistication, not because it solves a real business problem. Kubernetes is genuinely powerful. It is also genuinely complex. Getting both of those truths in view at the same time is the only way to make a good decision about it.

Stop Worshipping DORA Metrics: The Uncomfortable Truth About DevOps Measurement

ML Design

What Software Engineering Taught Me About AI/ML — And Why We Keep Ignoring It

There’s a quiet arrogance in how many organisations approach AI and Machine Learning today. Teams rush to stand up models. Data scientists celebrate AUC scores in isolation. Executives talk about “AI transformation” while the systems quietly drift, decay, and deliver less than promised — sometimes silently, sometimes catastrophically. I’ve been building software systems long enough to recognise this pattern. We’ve seen it before. And we solved it — imperfectly, incrementally, but genuinely — with software engineering discipline. The uncomfortable truth? Everything that makes a software system fail in production makes an ML system fail in production too. The names change. The tools change. The core problems don’t. This is what software engineering taught me about AI/ML, why ignoring those lessons creates more problems than it solves, and how a framework I call O-MARS can help organisations build ML systems the same way mature engineering teams build reliable software.
